Definition

A literature review is an assessment of a body of research that addresses a research topic or area.

When conducting a literature review, it is important to have quality over quantity.

 Use the following scholarly publication sources to complete the literature review:

  • Peer reviewed journals
  • Non-peer reviewed journals
  • Magazines
  • Books
  • Scholarly monographs
  • Book and monograph chapters
  • Grey literature (e.g., unpublished studies such as government reports, white papers, dissertations, conference papers)
  • Internet resources

Purpose

A literature review:

  • Intends to establish context for the research study
  • Contains critical analysis of previous research studies
  • Sometimes contains non-research-based literature on the topic of investigation
  • Provides an analytical argument and draws conclusions (more than descriptive)
  • Enables researchers and others to focus on and use the literature in the field
  • Engages theories and establishes them in relation to the current work
  • Helps to develop the research question further
  • Provides context and foundations for the later findings produced by the study

Process

When writing a literature review, you can start with the following questions:

  • What do we already know about the proposed topic?
  • Has anyone else ever conducted the same study?
  • Has anyone else ever conducted a similar study?
  • How is the work situated when compared with completed works?
  • What do we still need to know about the proposed topic?
  • Why is this research worth doing in the light of other research already completed?
  • What assumptions about the topic are apparent in the literature?

It is a research journey with several steps:

  1. Framing a research question
  2. Searching relevant bodies of literature
  3. Managing search results
  4. Synthesizing the research literature
  5. Writing an assessment of the literature
  6. The process is iterative—as you gain understanding, you’ll return to earlier steps to rethink, refine, and rework your literature review.

Ultimately, a good literature review should: 

  1. Have an introduction that sets the stage:
    • Restates question
    • Identifies and briefly explains the domains and how these domains are relevant to the research question
    • Gives the reader a good notion of what is to follow in the literature review—it should provide a road map for each domain, in the order the domains are discussed.
  2. Be well-organized:
    • Identifies and examines sections/chunks/domains and sub sections relevant to the question clusters or topics/issues of research problem
    • Provides coverage of the issues and depth of understanding. Are these issues synthesized well across the pieces of literature and organized according to common themes, rather than a description of one article, then the next, etc.?
    • Provides a theoretical/conceptual framework(s) or domains OR organized by key issues and studies that surround the question
  3. Wrap up with a conclusion: 
    • Summarize key ideas and findings from the literature and apply its relevancy to your research question(s)
    • Set the stage for your data collection
Top Ten Tips for Literature Reviews:
  1. The review of literature should adequately cover previous research on the topic
  2. The review of literature should cite actual findings from other studies
  3. The review of literature should be up-to-date.
    • Should include the most recent research.
    • Older studies may be relevant as well.
    • Primary vs. secondary sources
  4. The review of literature should analyze as well as summarize previous studies.
    • May be  a critique of methodology or inappropriate generalizations, limitations of the study, or a discussion of conflicting results
  5. The review of literature should be organized logically by topic, not by author (synthesize!).
  6. The review of literature should briefly summarize minor   studies and discuss major studies in detail.
  7. The review of literature should relate previous studies explicitly to the research problem/question or methods.
  8. The review of literature may establish a theoretical or conceptual framework (concept or idea would work also) for the problem.
  9. Explain technical terms and avoid jargon. Be sure you have definitions when appropriate.
  10. Include visual displays, including tables, graphs are always a nice way to reduce/display the data in your literature review and easy for the eye to follow.
    • Summarize a list of studies/compare and contrast
    • Can use visuals from literature if cited
    • Also may use your own literature map (I like making one, even if I don’t use it in the lit review – helpful to those that are visual)
