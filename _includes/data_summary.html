Definition

Findings which are reduced and displayed appropriately. The Data Summary should: 

  • Include an introduction that lays out structure and content of data results and relevant analysis performed. 
  • Present and describes the data in an unbiased, accurate, complete manner. 
  • Present data/evidence that is appropriate (type and quality) to the research question. 
  • Present data/evidence that is reduced and displayed in a useful way.
  • Include Graphs and tables that are clear and contain appropriate labels.
  • If statistical analysis performed, include sufficient information to help the reader understand the analysis conducted. 
  • Include a brief conclusion that summarizes the data results (not discussion/interpretation).

Process

Questions to ask as you work with the data:

  • What questions are you trying to answer and what do you specifically want to show?
  • Who is the audience of this data?
  • What types of data do you have and what are the key relationships?

Data Visualization: Two Phases

Phase 1: What to Visualize

Consider your inputs

  • Your goals
    • What questions are you trying to answer?
  • Their needs
    • Who is the data consumer and what do you want them to know?
  • Shape of the data
    • What type of data are you working with?
      • Categorical (grouped - sex)
      • Ordinal (ranked - satisfaction)
      • Interval (scales, temperature)
      • Ratio (age, salary)
      • Relational (hierarchical)
Phase 2: How to Visualize

Organize your data with goals in mind

  • Show relationship of A and B?
  • Compare two points in time?
  • Compare/Contrast?
  • Table or Chart?

Tables vs. Charts/Graphs

  • Tables
    • To look up individual values
    • To compare individual values
    • When precise values are required
  • Charts
    • When the message is in the pattern
    • To revel relationships among multiple values
      • Use stacked-bar chart for part-to-whole relationships to present cumulative totals
      • Use bar graphs over pie charts to show groups comparison
      • Use a line graph if the x-axis can show interval data
      • Used a scatterplot to show correlations

Reporting Data Results

  • Be impartial in the write-up, avoid value-laden statements
  • Do not use first person, allow the readers to see the research through the data and their own eyes
  • Should be able to replicate the study and come to the same conclusions
  • Include representative samples for a theme
  • Not interesting or nonessential data should be in appendices, if appropriate
  • Use numerals greater than or equal to 10; dates, ages or time; reporting the participants, reporting scores
  • Using words for quantification – values less than 10, Numbers that begin sentences
  • Numerical data should be reported in descending order
  • Use tables and figures to organize data, but also refer to them in the text. Don’t forget to use APA format.
